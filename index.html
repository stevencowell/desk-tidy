Stage 4 Technology Mandatory – Materials & Production Processes

Desk Tidy Unit (10 weeks)

Unit Overview

This 10-week unit guides Year 7/8 students through the complete design and production of a timber Desk Tidy. From a real-world brief (organising pens, pencils, cables and paperclips) students research, ideate, plan, build and evaluate a functional wooden organiser while developing safe workshop practices and foundational woodworking skills.

Assessment

  • Practical project – Desk Tidy construction and finish (60%)
  • Safe work habits & OnGuard safety quiz (20%)
  • Design folio, working drawings & evaluation report (20%)

Syllabus Outcomes (Materials & Production Processes)

  • TE4-SDP-01 – Explains how sustainable material choices (e.g., radiata pine vs. MDF) influence Desk Tidy design and production.
  • TE4-PDP-01 – Describes designer and producer practices involved in creating workstation organisers.
  • TE4-MSC-01 – Analyses properties of timber and composite boards to select appropriate materials for Desk Tidy components.
  • TE4-DES-01 – Produces and evaluates sketches and working drawings for a Desk Tidy solution.
  • TE4-PPM-01 – Plans and manages a production schedule, cutting list and resource usage for the Desk Tidy build.
  • TE4-SAF-01 – Demonstrates safe selection and use of hand tools, power tools and protective equipment in the workshop.

Desk Tidy Unit

The Desk Tidy unit introduces students to timber basics through a short design and build project. Over 10 weeks they plan and construct a small organiser while learning safe workshop practice and design thinking.

Summary & Duration

Duration: 10 Weeks (40-minute lessons, 4 per week)

This Stage 4 project guides students through the design process from brief to evaluation. Main Theory Content

Week 1: Introduction and Workshop Safety

Introduction

Welcome to the Desk Tidy project! In this first week, we introduce the project and focus on workshop safety. A desk tidy is a small wooden organizer that keeps your desk items neat. We're going to design and build one over the next 10 weeks. Before any cutting or building, safety is our top priority – a workshop has tools and equipment that can be dangerous if not used properly.

Workshop Safety Basics

  • Listen to instructions: Pay close attention to your teacher and follow their guidance at all times.
  • Wear PPE (Personal Protective Equipment): Always wear your safety glasses or goggles when cutting, sanding, or using any tools. Put on a workshop apron to protect your clothes and keep loose clothing in check. Sturdy shoes must be worn.
  • Tie back long hair and remove jewelry so nothing gets caught in tools.
  • No running or goofing around in the workshop.
  • Use tools correctly: only after you have been taught how to use them safely.
  • Keep a tidy workspace and clean up any spills.
  • Be patient and never rush; ask the teacher if you feel unsure.
  • Report problems immediately if a tool breaks or someone is injured.

By following these rules, we can prevent accidents and ensure the workshop is a safe, fun place to create your desk tidy. Safety may seem like common sense, but even experienced woodworkers follow strict safety rules every day. Let’s build good habits from the start!

Week 2: Design Brief and the Design Process

Design Brief

A design brief is a short statement explaining what you will make and the needs it meets. For example, our desk tidy brief might be to build a compact organiser that holds pens, pencils, a sharpener and an eraser.

The Design Process

The process starts by identifying the problem, researching ideas, sketching several designs, then planning, building and finally evaluating the result. This week we focus on understanding the task and researching existing products so we can design better.

Week 3: Sketching and Developing Design Ideas

Now that you have a clear idea of the problem and have done some research, it's time to come up with design ideas for your desk tidy.

Start by sketching several concepts. They can be simple boxes with dividers, an L-shape for pens and sticky notes, or even a circular holder on a tray. Keep sketches rough and label key features and sizes.

Compare your ideas with the brief and criteria. Choose the design that meets requirements, is practical to build, and looks good. Combine the best parts of different sketches if needed.

Create a detailed drawing of the chosen design with measurements in millimetres. Show how pieces join so someone else could build it from your drawing.

By the end of the week you should have a favourite design and a clear sketch to guide construction. Ask classmates or your teacher for feedback before moving on.

Week 5: Measuring and Marking Out

Now we get hands-on: before any cutting, we need to accurately measure and mark our wood. Precision here means parts that fit well.

Units and Tools

We use millimeters (mm) for precision. Key tools include:

  • Ruler/Tape Measure: read carefully and note measurements in mm.
  • Try Square: draw accurate 90° lines from an edge.
  • Pencil: keep it sharp for fine marks.

Measuring Twice

"Measure twice, cut once" reminds us to double-check every mark before cutting.

Marking Out Steps

  1. Measure from a reference edge and mark a small tick.
  2. Repeat from another spot to confirm the distance.
  3. Join the marks with a try square.
  4. Mark an "X" on the waste side of the line.

Accuracy Tips

  • Start measuring from the ruler's zero mark.
  • Keep the ruler straight and aligned.
  • Clamp identical parts together when marking.
  • Use reference edges and templates when needed.
  • Have someone check your marks before cutting.

Week 6: Cutting and Assembly

This week we cut our marked pieces and begin joining them to build the desk tidy body.

Cutting Safely

  • Clamp timber firmly before sawing.
  • Use smooth strokes and keep hands clear of the blade.
  • Stay on the waste side of the line when cutting.

Assembly Basics

  1. Lay out all pieces and check sizes.
  2. Spread wood glue on joining surfaces.
  3. Hold pieces square with clamps until dry.
  4. Add nails or screws if needed for strength.

Week 7: Attaching Hardware

With the desk tidy assembled, this week focuses on attaching small hardware like handles or hooks. Correct placement and screw technique keep your project sturdy.

Key Points

  • Mark hardware positions carefully before drilling.
  • Drill pilot holes to stop the timber splitting.
  • Select screws just long enough to hold securely.
  • Countersink if you want screw heads level with the surface.
  • Use a screwdriver that fits the screw head properly.

Week 9: Finishing and Evaluating

Final Finishes

With all parts assembled, we sand the entire desk tidy and apply a protective finish such as clear varnish or furniture wax. Light sanding between coats keeps the surface smooth.

Evaluating Your Work

Check that joints line up neatly, the desk tidy stands without wobbling, and the surfaces feel even. Decide whether the design keeps your supplies organised and note any improvements for next time.

Write a short evaluation describing what worked well and what you would change if you built it again.

Week 10: Finishing Touches and Presentation

In the final week, give your desk tidy a smooth finish and prepare to show the class what you have made. Sand any rough surfaces, apply paint or clear sealer in thin coats, and make sure every joint is secure. When presenting, speak clearly about your design choices and what you learned. Afterwards, tidy the workspace for the next class.

Finishing Steps

  • Sand surfaces smooth and remove all dust before finishing.
  • Apply finish in light coats, letting each coat dry fully.
  • Check that hardware is tight and glue joints are solid.

Presentation Tips

Show your sketch or plan, point out key features, and mention any challenges you overcame while making the project.

Week 1: Advanced Content

Safety in a workshop isn’t just a classroom rule – it’s a professional requirement known as Work Health and Safety (WHS). Professionals follow strict safety protocols. Loud machinery may require ear protection and fine wood dust often means wearing a mask or using special ventilation. Around 2,000 U.S. workers suffer an eye injury each day, and most could be prevented with proper protection. This shows why wearing safety goggles is so important. In advanced workshops you may also see safety signs reminding everyone to wear PPE and be cautious.

Case Study: In one woodworking shop, a craftsman skipped wearing safety glasses for a quick cut. A small piece of wood flew up and permanently injured his eye. Since then, that shop enforces a rule that no one cuts anything without eyewear, even for a second.
