Subject Program

Stage 4 Technology Mandatory – Materials & Production Processes

Desk Tidy Unit (10 weeks)

Unit Overview

This 10-week unit guides Year 7/8 students through the complete design and production of a timber Desk Tidy. From a real-world brief (organising pens, pencils, cables and paperclips) students research, ideate, plan, build and evaluate a functional wooden organiser while developing safe workshop practices and foundational woodworking skills.

Assessment

  • Practical project – Desk Tidy construction and finish (60%)
  • Safe work habits & OnGuard safety quiz (20%)
  • Design folio, working drawings & evaluation report (20%)

Syllabus Outcomes (Materials & Production Processes)

  • TE4-SDP-01 – Explains how sustainable material choices (e.g., radiata pine vs. MDF) influence Desk Tidy design and production.
  • TE4-PDP-01 – Describes designer and producer practices involved in creating workstation organisers.
  • TE4-MSC-01 – Analyses properties of timber and composite boards to select appropriate materials for Desk Tidy components.
  • TE4-DES-01 – Produces and evaluates sketches and working drawings for a Desk Tidy solution.
  • TE4-PPM-01 – Plans and manages a production schedule, cutting list and resource usage for the Desk Tidy build.
  • TE4-SAF-01 – Demonstrates safe selection and use of hand tools, power tools and protective equipment in the workshop.

Main Theory Content

Week 5: Measuring and Marking Out

Now we get hands-on: before any cutting, we need to accurately measure and mark our wood. Precision here means parts that fit well.

Units and Tools

We use millimeters (mm) for precision. Key tools include:

  • Ruler/Tape Measure: read carefully and note measurements in mm.
  • Try Square: draw accurate 90° lines from an edge.
  • Pencil: keep it sharp for fine marks.

Measuring Twice

"Measure twice, cut once" reminds us to double-check every mark before cutting.

Marking Out Steps

  1. Measure from a reference edge and mark a small tick.
  2. Repeat from another spot to confirm the distance.
  3. Join the marks with a try square.
  4. Mark an "X" on the waste side of the line.

Accuracy Tips

  • Start measuring from the ruler's zero mark.
  • Keep the ruler straight and aligned.
  • Clamp identical parts together when marking.
  • Use reference edges and templates when needed.
  • Have someone check your marks before cutting.
Week 5 Quiz

  1. What is the main advantage of using millimeters over centimeters?




  2. You need to cut a piece 150 mm long. What should you do before cutting?




  3. What tool helps you draw a perpendicular (90°) line across a piece of wood?




  4. You have two identical side pieces to mark out. How can you ensure they are marked the same?




  5. What does the phrase "measure twice, cut once" mean?




Conclusion

Accurate measuring and marking are the foundation of a good project. Taking your time here means parts fit together properly and you avoid wasting wood.

Support Theory Content (Optional)

Week 1: Support Content

Safety first!

Always be safe when working with wood. Wear safety glasses and sturdy closed shoes. Keep the workshop tidy and put away pencils and tools after use. This keeps everyone safe and prevents trips or spills.

Measuring wood

We use a pencil and straight tools to mark wood before cutting. First, mark a "face" side and edge on your wood so all measurements start from the same spot. Remember: measure twice, cut once. When you saw, cut on the waste side of the line so the piece stays the right size.

Support Quiz – Week 1

  1. What should you always wear in the workshop?


Week 2: Measuring and Marking

Hard and soft wood

Wood can come from many trees. Hardwoods (like oak or mahogany) are strong and heavy. Softwoods (like pine or cedar) grow fast and are easier to cut. For example, pine is a softwood and is lighter and easier to saw than oak.

Drilling holes

We use a drill press to make holes for parts of the desk tidy. The drill spins a bit very fast through the wood. Always clamp the wood down, keep fingers away, and wear safety glasses when drilling.

Support Quiz – Week 2

  1. Which tool is best for drawing a 90-degree line across timber?


Week 3: Timber Types

Wood grain

Wood comes from trees. You can see rings in a tree cross-section; each ring is one year of growth. Knots are dark spots where branches grew. These patterns, called grain, can make wood pretty but also affect how easy it is to cut.

Drawing our plan

Before building, we make drawings of our desk tidy. Drawing helps us see the shape and size of pieces. We use rulers and triangles to draw straight lines. This way we know what to build before we start cutting.

Support Quiz – Week 3

  1. Which of these is a hardwood?


Week 4: Hand Tools

Joining wood pieces

This week we put the sides, front, and back of the desk tidy together. We cut small grooves so pieces fit neatly. We used glue and clamps to hold everything square and tight.

Tools and safety

We keep our tools sharp and clean so cuts are smooth and safe. After using a tool, we turn it off or unplug it. By taking care of our tools, we stay safe and the work looks better.

Support Quiz – Week 4

  1. Which tool cuts along the grain of wood?


Week 5: Joining Methods

Smoothing edges

We plane the top and bottom edges of the desk tidy parts at a 45° angle, making a chamfer. This means the edges are slanted and smooth. We always plane with the grain of the wood to avoid rough spots.

Gluing the desk tidy

Now we dry-fit all pieces and apply wood glue along the edges. We clamp the pieces together and check that everything is lined up before the glue dries. Once clamped, we let the glue harden so the desk tidy becomes one strong piece.

Support Quiz – Week 5

  1. Which joint is easiest for beginners?


Week 6: Finishing

Sanding and applying varnish or paint protect the timber and improve appearance.

Support Quiz – Week 6

  1. Why do we sand timber before finishing?
